Geoeconomic Risk as a Stochastic Process — the OPEC+ Case

Status: complete Data: BZ=F · 4,648 daily obs · 2000–2026 Language: Python 3.10 April 2025

When a cartel enforces price boundaries, Brownian motion has nothing to say about it. Standard GBM treats all price levels identically and all shocks as continuous. Neither is true for OPEC+-controlled commodity markets. This piece builds a mean-reverting jump-diffusion SDE from the economics up — and calibrates it on real data.

Why standard GBM fails here

Geometric Brownian Motion assumes constant drift and constant volatility, with no memory of where prices "should" be. For equity prices over long horizons, this is defensible — there is no structural reason for a stock to revert to a fixed level. For Brent crude under active OPEC+ management, it is wrong in two specific ways.

First, there is a systematic restoring force. OPEC+ member fiscal breakevens cluster near $70–$75. Below that range, members run budget deficits and have a strong collective incentive to cut production — continuously, and proportionally to how far below the floor prices are. Above $85–$90, US shale operators can profitably hedge forward production and rig counts rise within 3–6 months. The result is not a hard wall — it is a restoring force that grows stronger the further prices deviate from the band. This is precisely the structure of an Ornstein-Uhlenbeck drift.

Second, there are discrete geopolitical shocks: surprise OPEC+ production decisions, tanker route disruptions, sudden demand revisions. These arrive discontinuously and produce instantaneous price jumps that a continuous diffusion process cannot replicate. An explicit Poisson jump component is required.

Building the SDE term by term

The state variable is \(X(t) = \log(\text{Brent spot})\), working in log-price to ensure positivity and to make percentage moves the unit of analysis.

Drift. The restoring force argument translates directly to OU drift: \(\kappa(\theta - X)\,dt\). When \(X > \theta\) the force is negative; when \(X < \theta\) positive. The speed \(\kappa\) and the equilibrium level \(\theta\) are calibrated from data.

Diffusion. Market uncertainty is not uniform across the price band. Near $70 and $90, implied volatility is elevated — OPEC meetings and shale hedging reports are watched closely. In the middle of the band, markets are calm. This is encoded as \(\sigma_0(1 + \alpha|X - \theta|)\,dW\): baseline vol scaled by distance from the mean. The key consequence is that this nonlinearity breaks Fokker-Planck analytical tractability — the diffusion coefficient has a discontinuous derivative at \(X = \theta\) — which forces numerical treatment.

Jumps. Geopolitical shocks arrive as a Poisson process with intensity \(\lambda\). Each shock moves log-price by \(J \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu_j, \sigma_j^2)\).

Core SDE — Assembled
\[ dX = \underbrace{\kappa(\theta - X)\,dt}_{\text{OPEC+ restoring force}} + \underbrace{\sigma_0(1 + \alpha|X - \theta|)\,dW}_{\text{state-dependent diffusion}} + \underbrace{J\,dq}_{\text{geopolitical jump}} \]

Analytical tractability — why numerical methods are necessary

Before building the numerical solver, the correct step is to attempt the analytical path and identify exactly where it fails. The diffusion coefficient \(\sigma(X) = \sigma_0(1 + \alpha|X - \theta|)\) is nonlinear in \(X\), with a discontinuous derivative at \(X = \theta\). The Fokker-Planck PDE for the transition density has a non-smooth diffusion term and admits no closed-form solution. The Euler-Maruyama discretisation is therefore not a shortcut — it is the only viable route.

Analytical tractability is a formal step in the methodology, not an afterthought. Classical OU with constant \(\sigma\) has a closed-form solution and a numerical solver is never needed. The moment \(\sigma\) becomes a function of \(X\), the Gaussian structure breaks. This case is deliberately constructed around that boundary — understanding exactly why the solver exists is what makes it transferable to more complex cases.

Key result

Running 10,000 Monte Carlo paths from \(X_0 = \log(82)\), horizon \(T = 0.5\) years:

The directional asymmetry is the primary departure from Black-Scholes. Starting below \(\theta = \$87.11\), the OU drift is positive — pulling price upward — making \(P(>\$90)\) roughly \(2\times P(<\$70)\) at 30 days (19.1% vs 9.2%). This directional bias is invisible to GBM, which has no concept of structural levels. The SDE also produces measurably heavier tails than GBM: at 90 days, the 5th–95th percentile range is \$57–\$113 under the SDE versus \$64–\$106 under GBM, at the same baseline volatility.
