USD/TRY Under Sanctions Shock — Why Jump Intensity Cannot Be Constant

Status: complete Data: USD/TRY · 5,459 daily obs · 2000–2026 Language: Python 3.10 June 2025

Case 01 treated jump intensity as a fixed parameter — the right assumption for Brent crude, where geopolitical shocks arrive with no particular clustering in time. For the Turkish lira, that assumption is structurally wrong. Sanctions announcements, central bank governor firings, and OFAC designations arrive in identifiable windows, and the data shows it. This case asks a different question: not what is the distribution of the exchange rate, but how does that distribution change when geopolitical tension is elevated?

Two structural departures from Case 01

The first departure is in the drift. Case 01 used an Ornstein-Uhlenbeck restoring force because OPEC+ actively defends a price band — there is a genuine mean to revert toward. USD/TRY has no such anchor. The lira has been on a one-way structural depreciation since 2018: chronic inflation differentials running 40–85% above US CPI, repeated episodes of unorthodox monetary policy, and persistent current account deficits. Fitting OU to this series would produce a historically distorted equilibrium level that pulls simulations in the wrong direction. The correct drift is a constant \(\mu\) — a GBM trend encoding the structural depreciation rate, with no reversion component at all. This is not a simplification; it is the economically honest description.

The second departure is more important. In Case 01, the Poisson jump process had a fixed intensity \(\lambda\) — one number describing the average arrival rate of geopolitical shocks across all market conditions. For Brent crude that is defensible. For TRY it is not. Three documented windows produced unmistakable clustering: the 2018 Brunson crisis where OFAC sanctions and tariff doubling caused TRY to lose 40% in weeks; the 2021 central bank shock where a single presidential decision to fire the rate-hiking governor caused a 15% single-session move; and the post-election uncertainty of 2023. A constant \(\lambda\) would treat 2016 — a relatively quiet year — identically to 2018. The data contradicts this.

What time-varying intensity means structurally

The solution is to let \(\lambda\) depend on an external geopolitical signal \(s(t) \in \{0,1\}\), where 0 is calm and 1 is elevated tension. The relationship is linear:

State-dependent jump intensity
\[ \lambda(t) = \lambda_0 + (\lambda_1 - \lambda_0)\cdot s(t) \]

In calm periods \(s=0\) and \(\lambda = \lambda_0\); under tension \(s=1\) and \(\lambda = \lambda_1\). The signal itself evolves as a two-state Markov chain — each day there is a small probability of transitioning from calm to tension, and a separate probability of returning to calm. This is not decorative complexity. It encodes the real structure of geopolitical risk: tension states are persistent (crises last weeks, not hours) but not permanent.

The full SDE assembles as:

Core SDE — Assembled
\[ dX = \underbrace{\mu\,dt}_{\text{structural depreciation}} + \underbrace{\sigma\,dW}_{\text{continuous FX noise}} + \underbrace{J\,dq(\lambda(t))}_{\text{state-dependent jump}} \]

where \(X(t) = \log(\text{USD/TRY})\), and jump sizes \(J \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu_j, \sigma_j^2)\) with \(\mu_j > 0\) encoding the depreciation bias — sanctions depreciate TRY; relief rallies are smaller and shorter-lived.

Why Merton's formula no longer applies

In Case 01, the jump process had constant \(\lambda\), and Merton's closed-form solution for jump-diffusion processes was available in principle (though rejected on other grounds). Here it is categorically unavailable. Merton's formula requires evaluating the characteristic function of the log-price process, which depends on \(\int_0^T \lambda(t)\,dt\). When \(\lambda(t)\) switches between \(\lambda_0\) and \(\lambda_1\) according to an unpredictable signal path, this integral has no closed form. The number of jumps over \([0,T]\) is no longer Poisson with a fixed parameter — it depends on the entire realised history of \(s(t)\). Euler-Maruyama is not a computational convenience here; it is the only viable route.

The new design pattern in Case 02 is that the signal generator lives inside the solver and advances its state at every time step. In Case 01, \(\lambda\) was set once. Here, before drawing each Poisson increment, the solver first queries the current signal state, computes the current \(\lambda(t)\), then draws the jump. The signal is shared across all simulation paths — it represents a market-wide geopolitical condition, not a path-specific random variable. This architecture carries directly into Case 04's regime-switching Markov chain, where the state variable drives not just jump intensity but the entire drift-diffusion structure.

What the calibration reveals about TRY's jump character

Calibrating on 5,459 daily observations from January 2000 to January 2026, with days where \(|\text{return}| > 2\sigma\) classified as jumps and removed before fitting the continuous parameters, produces a picture that is distinct from Brent crude. The drift \(\mu = 16.6\%\)/yr is large and positive — structural depreciation is not a small perturbation. The baseline jump intensity \(\lambda_0 = 8.96\)/yr means approximately nine shock events per year even in calm conditions. Under tension, \(\lambda_1 = 26.87\)/yr — three times higher. But the individual jump size \(\sigma_j = 4.1\%\) is modest. TRY's risk profile is frequent small shocks rather than rare catastrophic ones, consistent with its history of chronic politically-driven volatility rather than occasional existential crises.

The result and why it matters for positioning

Running 10,000 paths from USD/TRY = 38, the structural drift dominates both regimes — \(P(\text{depreciation} > 5\%)\) exceeds 80% at the 90-day horizon regardless of signal state. The regime difference is not in the center of the distribution but in the tail: the 95th percentile shifts from 69.4 under calm to 71.6 under tension. That is a 3.2% move at the extreme, for a position sized in notional terms.

This is the characteristic signature of increased jump intensity — more frequent shocks accumulate into a heavier extreme tail while the median path changes little. A standard model inflating overall implied volatility to match elevated tension would contaminate the central distribution and misprice at-the-money options. The regime-conditional model isolates the tail shift cleanly. A trader who believes tension is elevated should pay more for topside USD/TRY optionality relative to downside — and this model quantifies exactly how much more.
