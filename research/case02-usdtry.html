USD/TRY Under Sanctions Shock — Time-Varying Jump Intensity and Geopolitical Tension

Status: complete Data: USD/TRY · 5,459 daily obs · 2000–2026 Language: Python 3.10 June 2025

The Turkish lira does not drift smoothly — it depreciates persistently, then breaks sharply when geopolitical tension escalates. Case 01 treated jump intensity as a fixed constant. That is wrong for a currency where sanctions announcements, central bank firings, and OFAC designations cluster in identifiable windows. This case makes jump intensity time-varying, driven by an external geopolitical signal, and asks a structurally different question: how does the tail of the USD/TRY distribution change between calm and tension regimes?

Why constant jump intensity fails here

Case 01's OU drift was the economically honest choice for Brent — there is a structural restoring force. That same logic, applied here, leads to a different conclusion. USD/TRY has been on a one-way structural depreciation since 2018, driven by chronic inflation differentials (Turkish CPI persistently 40–85% above US CPI), repeated unorthodox monetary policy episodes, and persistent current account deficits. There is no fixed long-run level for TRY to revert toward. Fitting OU to this series would produce a historically distorted equilibrium that pulls simulations in the wrong direction. The correct drift is constant GBM — encoding the structural trend, not a reversion.

The more important departure from Case 01 is in the jump process. Three documented escalation windows produced measurable clustering of large TRY moves: the 2018 Brunson crisis (OFAC sanctions and tariff doubling; TRY lost 40% in weeks), the 2021 central bank shock (Erdogan fired the governor who raised rates; TRY fell 15% in a single session), and post-election uncertainty in 2023. A constant \(\lambda\) treats 2016–2017 identically to 2018 — clearly wrong. The model must allow jump intensity to switch between a baseline calm level and an elevated tension level.

Building the SDE term by term

The state variable is \(X(t) = \log(\text{USD/TRY})\). An increase means lira depreciation; the starting value is \(X_0 = \log(38)\).

Drift. No long-run mean exists for TRY — OU is rejected on economic grounds. Constant GBM drift encodes the structural depreciation trend: \(\mu(X,t) = \mu\), estimated as the mean annualised log-return from the clean (jump-removed) series.

Diffusion. Continuous FX noise from portfolio flows, bid-ask dynamics, and routine trading. Constant \(\sigma\) is appropriate; extreme volatility is captured by the jump term.

Jump. Sanctions and political shocks arrive as a Poisson process with time-varying intensity \(\lambda(t)\) driven by geopolitical signal \(s(t) \in \{0,1\}\): \[\lambda(t) = \lambda_0 + (\lambda_1 - \lambda_0)\cdot s(t)\] where \(\lambda_0\) is the calm-period intensity and \(\lambda_1\) the elevated tension intensity. Jump sizes follow \(J \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu_j, \sigma_j^2)\) with \(\mu_j > 0\) encoding the depreciation bias — sanctions depreciate TRY; relief rallies are smaller and shorter-lived.

Core SDE — Assembled
\[ dX = \underbrace{\mu\,dt}_{\text{structural depreciation}} + \underbrace{\sigma\,dW}_{\text{continuous FX noise}} + \underbrace{J\,dq(\lambda(t))}_{\text{state-dependent jump}} \]

Analytical tractability — why the solver is unavoidable

The drift change from OU to constant \(\mu\) does not affect tractability — a constant drift is trivially integrable. The binding constraint is the jump term. Merton's closed-form formula requires constant \(\lambda\). The moment \(\lambda(t) = \lambda_0 + (\lambda_1-\lambda_0)\cdot s(t)\) becomes time-varying, the jump process is non-stationary. Evaluating the characteristic function requires computing \(\int_0^T \lambda(t)\,dt\), and when \(s(t)\) switches state unpredictably this integral has no closed form. The number of jumps over \([0,T]\) is no longer Poisson with a fixed parameter — it depends on the entire path of \(s(t)\). Euler-Maruyama is the only viable route.

The new numerical technique in Case 02 is not a different solver — it is a different solver design. In Case 01, \(\lambda\) was passed in once and never changed. Here, \(\lambda(t)\) is re-evaluated at every time step from the current signal state before drawing the Poisson increment. The signal \(s(t)\) itself evolves as a two-state Markov chain — calm to tension with probability \(p_\text{escalate}\) per day, tension to calm with probability \(p_\text{de-escalate}\) — and this state generator lives inside the model object and advances on every solver call. This design pattern carries directly into Case 04's regime-switching Markov chain.

TRY's jump character is frequent small moves rather than rare catastrophic ones — 9–27 events per year at 4% each, consistent with its history of chronic politically-driven volatility. The \(\lambda_1/\lambda_0 = 3\) ratio is prior-based; separate calm/tension estimation was attempted but produced \(\lambda_1 = 0\) due to sparse coverage in Yahoo Finance tension windows.

Key result — regime signal is in the tail, not the center

Running 10,000 Monte Carlo paths from \(X_0 = \log(38)\), horizon \(T = 1\) year, under both signal states:

The constant drift \(\mu = 16.6\%\)/yr drives both median paths upward from 38 — depreciation dominates in either regime, with \(P(\text{dep}>5\%)\) exceeding 80% at the 90-day horizon. The regime difference is not in the center of the distribution but in the tail: the 95th percentile is 71.6 under tension versus 69.4 under calm — a 3.2% shift at the extreme. For a position sized in notional terms, that tail difference is material. The tension regime delivers 3× more shock events per unit time; a standard model would need to inflate overall implied vol to match this, which distorts the central distribution. The model isolates the tail shift without contaminating the median.
