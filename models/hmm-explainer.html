How a Hidden Markov Model Infers What It Cannot See

A self-contained demonstration of HMM forward filtering — no real data required. The model watches noisy signals arrive one at a time and infers the hidden state generating them. At the end, the truth is revealed.

The locked room

You are locked in a room with no windows. Every few seconds, a piece of paper slides under the door with a single number on it. You cannot see outside. You cannot ask questions. You can only read the numbers as they arrive.

But you know something useful from past experience. You know that the weather outside follows a pattern — it tends to stay the same for a while before changing — and you know roughly what numbers each type of weather tends to produce. Sunny days tend to produce small numbers. Cloudy days produce medium ones. Stormy days produce large ones. The numbers are noisy — a sunny day might occasionally slide you a 7, a stormy day might send a 2 — but the pattern is real.

Your job: after each new number, update your best guess about what the weather outside actually is. That is exactly what a Hidden Markov Model does. The weather is the hidden state. The numbers are the observations. Your updating process is the forward algorithm.

What the forward algorithm is computing

At each step the model maintains a belief vector — one probability per state. When a new observation \(x_t\) arrives, the update has two parts.

Predict: multiply the current belief through the transition matrix to account for possible state changes since the last step. States are sticky — the model expects the weather to persist — so this step barely moves the belief.

Update: for each state \(k\), evaluate how likely the new observation is under that state's emission distribution \(\mathcal{N}(\mu_k, \sigma_k^2)\). Multiply the predicted probability by that likelihood, then normalise so all three probabilities sum to one. This is Bayes' rule applied one step at a time.

The lag you observe at transitions — the model staying confident in the old state for 2–3 steps after the truth has changed — is the correct Bayesian behaviour. A single anomalous observation should not immediately override a strong prior. Evidence must accumulate before the belief shifts. In your paper, this same algorithm was applied to four financial features with parameters estimated from 20 years of market data.
